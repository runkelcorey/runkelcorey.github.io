In Vertigo (1958), an Uncertain History

Last updated on Jul 3, 2020 8 min read analysis, film

“We see now that the abyss of history is deep enough to hold us all.“

Vertigo places a thoroughly modern Scottie Ferguson1 within a thoroughly modern San Francisco. It then watches as he slips from his mental moorings into a twisted version of himself. His cycle of psychological terror constitutes a violent displacement, and it re-opens two key questions of belonging: of Scottie to San Francisco; and of San Francisco to Scottie. I will examine only the former today; the latter I leave for later.

My argument runs like this: Vertigo’s early scenes depict a San Francisco that is tailored to mobile white men like Scottie. For his part, Scottie enforced the modern status quo as a San Francisco cop, until his partner died trying to save Scottie from falling during a rooftop chase. With the danger supposedly over, he finds himself navigating a comfortable and controlled city, with sights/sites located just off the highway—Fort XX, the Muir Woods, and the Mission San Juan Bautista.

But these parks did not just happen. Public works efforts had to make the sites palettable to professionals and tourists. These efforts had to isolate the visual cues of danger from any actual, Wild West–type danger. Rather than places to be reckoned with, these are sights to be gawked at. In the first formulation, the site—with all its natural and political forces—participates with those who visit it; in the second, only the visitor is active, while the sight is frozen in space.

Vertigo exposes how thin this veneer of safety is. Between Madeleine’s actions and Scottie’s vertigo, the film reintroduces the distinct possibilities of danger to these places. In doing so, it unsettles the stable relationship between Scottie the subject and San Francisco the object.

The sublime, rather than an instrument which rattles people from their parochial understandings of history to glean a new future, turns them toward a never-ending past.

Vertigo is often viewed as the modernist movie. Here instead it is an early example of postmodernism.

Many of Vertigo’s lasting images star San Francisco as much as they do Jimmy Stewart or Kim Novak. Consider for example the massive pylons supporting the Golden Gate Bridge, the looming trunks of Muir Woods, or the towering figure cut by the Mission San Juan Bautista. These are objects so huge that Hitchcock’s camera cannot swallow them entirely, their vastness bleeding over the frame’s edges. And each of them repeat vertical features—the International Orange suspension cables, endless Sequoia sempervirens, late-afternoon shadows cast by pale white arches—that recede into the distance.

“Succession and uniformity of parts are what constitute the artificial infinite” wrote Edmund Burke in A Philosophical Enquiry Into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful, one of the first modern explorations of the sublime. He described the sublime as a state when “the mind is so entirely filled with its object, that it cannot entertain any other,” inciting terror and astonishment at its heights, while “the inferior effects are admiration, reverence and respect.” The treatment Hitchcock gives to San Francisco’s iconic sights meets Burke’s standard. These images represent a particular kind of sublime associated with a particular kind of political project. While great magnitude and manifold features are part of Burke’s sublime, he later disavows “colonnades and avenues of trees […] suffered to run to immense distances” as urbane. However, the ultimate height of construction (and of trees) was not foreseen by Burke in 1757: the technical and natural discoveries in nineteenth-century and early-twentieth-century America would dwarf those of eighteenth-century Europe. Moreover, metropolitans worldwide realized the need to incorporate great sights into their cityscapes. Historian David Nye writes of an American technological sublime, a fusion of American technical mastery and pre-American resources.

I argue that this modification of Burke’s typology (which had separated the sublime into natural and architectural modes) can be used to better understand how the expansive imagery in Vertigo contributes to a “weakening of historicity, both in our relationship to public History and in the new forms of our private temporality”.^[Frederic Jameson, The Cultural Logic of Late Capitalism Keeping with my earlier analysis of the film, I maintain that Vertigo presents both a private message concerning Scottie Ferguson2 and a public message about the social geography of 1950’s America. In the interest of clarity, I focus today only on the public message, detailing how the American sublime clashes pre-modernity with modernity, disorienting Scottie’s experience of history.

What drives Scottie?

Understanding the sublime images in Vertigo begins with understanding how Scottie encounters such pictures. Since the film is shot (mostly) from his perspective, Hitchcock’s treatment of the Golden Gate, the Muir Woods, and the Mission all derive from Scottie. While his desires have been the subject of several careers in film studies, it will help to simplify by looking at the film itself for direction. Stewart’s body language clues us in to the gravity of his encounter with Gavin Elster, Madeleine’s husband. As Nerdwriter demonstrates, this meeting has so much to do with each man’s power. All the mechanisms by which 1950s San Francisco distances itself from its Wild West past—the long sequences in sleek automobiles, the infrequent references to psychiatry, a brassiere operating by “the principle of the cantilever bridge”—are here disavowed in favor of a dangerous and unfamiliar past. That bra symbolizes surprisingly much of the city’s changes. Its “revolutionary uplift”4 are designed for the pleasure of men, as much of city’s attractions were. Meanwhile, the comfort provided by the innovative architecture undoubtedly improved women’s lives, albeit in small ways.

By including men and women, the bra is thus a factor of the democratic virtues of the American technological sublime outlined by David Nye. But it is also a product of that historical concept, designed by an engineer in his spare time, a “kind of do-it-yourself thing,” as Scottie notes. This spirit of tinkering that came from outside the state apparatus and wealthy class is the reason why “Engineers, builders, and inventors began to occupy […] the pantheon of republicanism.“5 The bra (by its inventor, its target audience, its cantilever design) symbolizes how invention, innovation, and ambitious construction catered to America’s middle class. These criteria of the American sublime would have been alien to a pre-modern San Francisco.

However, the above is but the first move in Nye’s distinctive double action of the American sublime. Where that first move “usurped the place of natural things with man-made objects” conceived as “internal improvements”, the second move covered the tracks of the first, assimilating the object of improvement as once again natural.
